# Presentation Q&A Prep: CFO and Dana
**For:** Loyalty Program Manager
**Date:** March 2026

---

## How to Read This Doc

CFO questions are harder — focused on numbers, risk, and ROI. Dana questions are softer — focused on brand fit, execution, and team capacity. Both sets are below.

For each question: read the **answer**, note the **watch out**, and practice saying it out loud before the meeting.

---

## CFO Questions

---

**"The program has already lost money. Why should we invest more?"**

> The $40,000 we've spent tells us exactly what doesn't work: points-based campaigns with no personality. Every dollar we spent confirmed the root cause. This proposal isn't more of the same — it's a fundamentally different approach. The quiz prototype is already built at no additional cost. We're asking for $28,000 to integrate and launch something we know works.

*Watch out: Don't be defensive about the previous investment. Own it and move on.*

---

**"These projections seem optimistic. What are they based on?"**

> The conservative case — the one I'm using — assumes we reach 6,500 MAU by month 12, which is below our stated target of 8,000. It assumes 2.8 visits per month, not 4.0. I deliberately modeled below target so the numbers are defensible. The comparable I used is Roast & Co., a specialty coffee chain that saw 18-point retention improvement within 90 days of launching a personalization feature.

*Watch out: Don't get pulled into defending the optimistic scenario. Always redirect to conservative.*

---

**"What's the payback period?"**

> At the conservative scenario — $82,000 total 12-month cost against $680,000 in gross profit uplift — we're net positive by month 3 or 4. The quiz integration cost is front-loaded; once it's live, the ongoing cost is $4,500 a month, which is covered by incremental visits from even a small improvement in retention.

*Watch out: Have this number memorized. Fumbling it will undermine confidence in the whole model.*

---

**"Why will this work when 10 other campaigns didn't?"**

> Every previous campaign was a discount mechanic — double points, bonus points, temporary promotions. They worked exactly as discount mechanics work: temporary spikes, no lasting behavior change. This isn't a campaign. It's a structural change to what the program is. We're not offering a better deal. We're giving members an identity. That's what every successful loyalty program — Starbucks, Dutch Bros, Roast & Co. — is actually built on.

*Watch out: Don't disparage the previous manager. Frame it as an industry-wide mistake, not a personal one.*

---

**"What if members take the quiz once and forget about it?"**

> That's what the personalization engine is for. The quiz result isn't the end — it's the beginning. Once we know someone is a Zen Minimalist, every push notification, every drink recommendation, every challenge is tailored to them. The quiz is the mechanism that makes the rest of the program personal. Without it, we're back to blasting everyone the same message.

---

**"Can't we just fix the push notifications and birthday rewards and call it done?"**

> We can fix those — and we should, they're on the plan. But fixing broken automations won't change the program NPS. People aren't indifferent because the birthday email didn't send. They're indifferent because the program has no personality. The fixes are table stakes. The quiz is what actually changes the relationship.

---

**"What's the risk if this doesn't hit projections?"**

> The risk is that we're back where we are today — spending $4,500 a month on a program with 2,100 active users. But even that scenario is better than cancellation: we preserve the loyalty infrastructure, the member data, and the option to iterate. The floor is the current state. The upside is significant. And the prototype is already working — this isn't a bet on an untested idea.

---

**"Why do we need $15,000 for quiz integration? The prototype is already built."**

> The prototype is the front-end experience — the quiz itself. The $15,000 covers connecting it to the rewards app backend: storing personality tags against member profiles, building the recommendation engine, and triggering personalized campaigns based on results. Without that integration, the quiz is a standalone experience with no connection to the loyalty program. The $15,000 is what makes it actually change behavior.

---

## Dana Questions

---

**"How does this fit the Basecamp brand?"**

> It IS the Basecamp brand, expressed digitally. Warm, knowledgeable, community-focused — a coffee personality experience is exactly what a barista does at the counter every day. We're just giving the app the ability to do the same thing. No competitor has done this. It fits us more naturally than it would fit anyone else.

---

**"What's the rollout look like for store managers? They're already frustrated."**

> We've kept it simple for them. The barista training guide is one page — it covers the four personalities, when to mention the quiz, and what to say. No scripts, no hard sell. We're asking baristas to mention it naturally when the moment is right, the same way they'd recommend a drink. Jennifer coordinates the logistics. Marcus Jr. at downtown can help pilot the training so it comes from a peer, not HQ.

---

**"How are we driving social sharing?"**

> Two ways. First, the quiz results are inherently shareable — people want to share their personality type. We're building branded result cards for Instagram Stories (Month 3). Second, we're relaunching Refer a Friend with quiz sharing as the hook: share your result, your friend gets a welcome drink. That's acquisition built into the experience.

---

**"What does success look like at 30 days?"**

> Three things: quiz completion rate at 30%+ of active members, push notification open rate recovering to 12%+ (from 5%), and early retention data showing quiz completers visiting more often than non-completers. That last one is the key signal — if people who take the quiz come back more, the whole model holds.

---

**"What do we say publicly about this? How do we announce it?"**

> We let the quiz speak for itself. The launch message is simple: "Discover your coffee personality." No announcement about a program refresh, no corporate language. Just the experience. People share it because it's fun and personal — not because we told them to.

## If the Meeting Goes Sideways

**If the CFO says "we need more time to review the numbers":**
> "Of course. The full business case is in the doc I'll send after this meeting. The one thing I'd ask is that we set a follow-up within the week — every month we wait is another month at 27% retention."

**If someone says "let's table this for next quarter":**
> "I want to flag the risk of that. Satisfaction is declining month over month. At the current trajectory, we'll have fewer active members by next quarter than we do today. The 90-day clock starts when we get the green light — I want to make sure we have enough runway to show results before the year is out."

**If there's silence after the ask:**
> Let it sit. Don't fill it. The ask is on the table. Wait for them to respond.
